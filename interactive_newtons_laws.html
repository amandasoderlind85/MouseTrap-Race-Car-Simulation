Newton's Three Laws of Motion
Read each law, then find its connection to your mousetrap car.
1

First Law — The Law of Inertia

An object at rest stays at rest, and an object in motion stays in motion at the same speed and in the same direction — unless acted on by an unbalanced force.
🪤 Mousetrap car connection: Before the spring releases, the car sits perfectly still. It has inertia at rest — it won't move until the spring provides an unbalanced force. After the spring finishes pulling, the car keeps rolling (inertia in motion) until friction — an unbalanced force — gradually stops it.
2

Second Law — F = ma

The acceleration of an object depends on the net force acting on it and its mass: F = m × a
🪤 Mousetrap car connection: The mousetrap spring provides the force (F). The car's mass is m. A stronger spring → more force → more acceleration. A heavier car → more mass → less acceleration for the same force. This is exactly what you'll test in the simulator!

Try it: If your car has a mass of 0.15 kg and accelerates at 2 m/s², the net force = 0.15 × 2 = 0.30 N.
3

Third Law — Action & Reaction

For every action force, there is an equal and opposite reaction force. Forces always come in pairs — acting on different objects.
🪤 Mousetrap car connection: When the spring releases, it pushes backward on the axle string (action). The string pulls forward on the spring arm (reaction) — propelling the car. The forces are equal and opposite, but they act on different objects, so they don't cancel out.

Real-world examples: Rocket engines push exhaust gas backward → the rocket accelerates forward. A swimmer pushes water backward → the swimmer moves forward.
